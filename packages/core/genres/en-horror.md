## Genre Taboos

- Jump scares as a substitute for actual dread — prose horror must build atmospheric tension
- Explaining the monster too early or too completely — mystery IS the horror
- MC making obviously stupid decisions just to advance the plot ("Let's split up!")
- Gore without purpose — violence must serve the story's emotional impact
- The horror has no rules — even cosmic horror needs internal consistency
- Characters who don't react realistically to traumatic events
- Evil for evil's sake — the best horror makes you understand why it's terrifying on a human level
- "It was all a dream" or similar copout endings

## Building Dread in Prose

**Horror in prose is psychological. You can't rely on visuals or music.**
- Wrongness, not monstrousness: the door that's always been on the left of the hallway is now on the right. No one else notices
- Sensory disconnection: a sound that shouldn't exist, a smell with no source, a shadow that moves independently
- Normalcy disrupted: the most effective horror starts in completely ordinary settings
- Pacing is everything: slow the prose down during tense moments. Short sentences. Single-line paragraphs. White space as silence
- The unseen is scarier than the seen: what the character IMAGINES is worse than what you can describe

## Cosmic Horror Specifics (if applicable)

- The threat is incomprehensible, not just powerful. Human minds can't FULLY understand it
- Knowledge is dangerous: the more you learn, the more your sanity erodes
- Victory is survival or acceptance, not defeat of the cosmic entity
- Scale matters: the entity doesn't hate humanity — it doesn't even notice humanity
- Madness is not a superpower — it's a tragedy

## Character Survival Logic

- Characters should make reasonable decisions based on available information
- Fear is rational — show characters being scared AND still making choices
- Different characters react to horror differently: fight, flight, freeze, rationalize, deny
- Death should mean something — even in horror with high body counts
- The survivor(s) are changed by the experience — they don't just "go back to normal"

## Atmosphere as Character

- Setting IS a character in horror: the house, the town, the forest, the ship
- Weather, lighting, sound — use environment to externalize internal dread
- Isolation (physical or psychological) amplifies horror
- Time distortion: hours feel like minutes, minutes feel like hours
- Familiar places becoming unfamiliar — the uncanny is horror's greatest tool
