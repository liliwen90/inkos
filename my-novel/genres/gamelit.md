## Genre Taboos

- Game mechanics that only apply when convenient for the MC
- "It's just a game" — stakes must exist (real-world consequences, meaningful in-game investment, community)
- NPCs with zero agency or humanity (if relevant to the story type)
- Pay-to-win elements presented positively with zero critique
- MC is instantly the best at everything — specialization creates interesting limitations
- Player community that acts nothing like real gaming communities
- Ignoring the social dynamics of guilds, parties, and online communities
- Zero consequences for PvP (no grudges, no reputation, no escalation)

## What Separates GameLit from LitRPG

**GameLit = game world is central, but stats/numbers are NOT the focus**
- Story focuses on social dynamics, strategy, community building, competition
- The game world creates unique situations that couldn't exist in regular fantasy
- Real-world stakes may or may not be present
- The "game" aspect is the setting, not the progression system

## Game World Design

- The game must feel like a real game that real people would play
- Economy, meta-game, exploits, patches — real game concepts drive plot
- Different player types (casual, hardcore, social, competitive) create natural conflict
- Game mechanics should create moral dilemmas: is corpse-camping the rival guild ethical?
- Developer decisions (patches, events, shutdowns) as external forces

## Real-World Integration

- Who are the players behind the avatars? What do they sacrifice to play?
- Online relationships vs. real-world relationships: the tension and overlap
- Gaming culture references should be authentic but accessible to non-gamers
- Health, time management, addiction — the dark side of gaming as potential subplot
- Professional gaming, streaming, game development as career/life elements

## Community Dynamics

- Guilds are social organizations: leadership, drama, recruitment, betrayal
- Server-wide events create shared stories
- Mentorship between veteran and new players
- Forum culture, memes, and community inside-jokes as worldbuilding
