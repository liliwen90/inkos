## Genre Taboos

- MC is a lone wolf who never needs anyone — post-apocalypse is about community
- Infinite ammunition, fuel, and food with no supply chain
- Every survivor is either a saint or a raider — no moral complexity
- The apocalypse happened but nothing about society actually changed
- MC finds exactly what they need in every building they loot
- Violence without psychological consequences (PTSD, guilt, nightmares)
- Women as objects to be "protected" rather than active survivors
- Ignoring disease, infection, sanitation, and exposure as real threats

## Survival Realism

**The apocalypse doesn't care about heroes. It cares about calories, clean water, and shelter.**
- Resources are finite and finding them is hard work, not adventure shopping
- Medical knowledge is worth more than combat skill
- Seasons matter: winter without heating is lethal, summer without clean water is lethal
- Infrastructure decay: roads crack, buildings collapse, bridges fail — the world is actively deteriorating
- Knowledge preservation: who remembers how to farm? how to purify water? how to deliver a baby?

## Social Dynamics After Collapse

- Power structures reform immediately — nature abhors a vacuum
- Every community faces the cooperation vs. exploitation dilemma
- Trade exists because no one settlement can produce everything
- Justice without courts: how do communities handle theft, murder, disputes?
- Children growing up post-apocalypse have fundamentally different worldview from pre-apocalypse adults

## The Apocalypse Itself

- Define it clearly: plague, nuclear, alien, supernatural, technological collapse, climate
- The cause shapes the aftermath: nuclear = radiation zones + mutations, plague = empty cities with intact infrastructure, etc.
- Ongoing threats from the original cause (aftershocks, continued exposure, recurring waves)
- The transition period (first weeks/months) vs. established post-apocalypse (years later) tells very different stories

## Moral Complexity

- Good people make terrible choices under pressure — that's the point
- "Raiders" had families, values, and reasons before they became desperate
- Hoarding resources = survival OR selfishness? The line blurs
- When do you help a stranger vs. protect your own? This question has no right answer
