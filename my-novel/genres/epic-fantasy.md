## Genre Taboos

- Good vs. Evil with zero moral complexity
- Prophecy that removes all agency (if the hero is destined to win, why read?)
- Medieval European setting with modern values and no cultural authenticity
- Magic system with no rules, costs, or limitations
- Travel sequences that ignore distance, weather, supplies, and fatigue
- Armies that function nothing like real military organizations
- "Chosen One" who wins through destiny, not character
- Female characters defined solely by their relationship to male characters

## World-Building at Scale

**Epic fantasy stands or falls on its world:**
- History must feel lived-in: ruins, cultural grudges, trade routes, linguistic drift
- Multiple cultures with genuinely different values, not just different hats
- Geography affects everything: trade, war, culture, religion, daily life
- Religion is political: gods (whether real or not) are tools of power
- Economy exists: who grows the food? who mines the iron? who profits from war?

## Magic System Design

**Sanderson's Laws as starting point, but not gospel:**
- The more magic solves plot problems, the more the reader must understand its rules
- Cost matters: the best magic systems make the cost as interesting as the effect
- Magic should feel different from technology — not just "science with fancy words"
- Cultural relationship to magic varies: some societies worship it, others fear it, others regulate it
- Magic should impact society in ways analogous to technology (what's the magical equivalent of the printing press?)

## Character Complexity

- Villains believe they're the heroes of their own story
- Allies disagree on methods even when aligned on goals
- Power corrupts — show the temptation, not just the resistance
- Characters from different cultures should genuinely THINK differently, not just have different accents
- Betrayal must be foreshadowed and motivated, never arbitrary

## Battle/War Realism

- Logistics matter: armies need food, rest, supply lines, command structure
- Morale is as important as strength: panic, desertion, rally
- Combat is exhausting, messy, and terrifying — heroism happens despite fear, not in absence of it
- Casualties have consequences: dead soldiers had families, communities
- Strategic thinking > individual heroics at large scale
