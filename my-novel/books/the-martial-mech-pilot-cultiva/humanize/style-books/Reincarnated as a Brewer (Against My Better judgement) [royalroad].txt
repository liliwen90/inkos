--- Chapter 1: Meeting God ---

The first thing he noticed was that the floor was wrong.
It was smooth beneath him, but not in any way he recognised. There was no grain to it, no join, no variation in texture. It felt less like stone or wood and more like the idea of a surface. Present, functional, and entirely uninterested in explaining itself.
That alone should have been alarming. What unsettled him more was that he could feel it at all.
He had no sense of weight. No sense of limbs. No breath to draw or release. And yet the floor existed, and so did his awareness of it.
He tried to blink. Nothing happened.
“Hello! Hi there! Welcome!”
The voice was cheerful. Bright. Too close.
It didn’t arrive through sound. It simply appeared, fully formed, inside his awareness.
His attention widened, as though something inside him had finally decided to render the rest of the scene.
A building stood before him.
It was wrong in the same way the floor was wrong, but louder about it.
The roof was tiled in a style he associated with old photographs of Japan, sloping neatly and casting sharp shadows. The pillars holding it up were unmistakably Roman, fluted and proportioned with mathematical confidence. Beneath them, the floor was polished wood, warm-toned, well-kept, the sort of thing that belonged in an English pub that took its atmosphere very seriously.
Each element made sense on its own.
Together, they did not.
Floating in front of the building was a girl.
She looked to be no more than eleven. Small, slight, with one knee bent, one leg straight but neither touching the floor as though gravity were a suggestion rather than a rule.
She wore a white robe clearly meant for someone older. It draped around her in excessive folds, heavy with ceremony and authority pressed into the fabric whether it suited her or not.
The fabric shimmered faintly, points of light glinting within it like distant stars. It moved despite the lack of wind, as though aware it was being observed.
The robe was not tailored so much as imposed, worn with the careful seriousness of a child playing a role they believed they were expected to take seriously. The effect wasn’t unsettling because of what it revealed, but because of what it tried and failed to be.
It felt rehearsed.
And, like the building behind her, it didn’t quite fit.
“Hi! So I’m God!” she said brightly. “Aren’t you impressed?”
He had the distinct impression that there was a correct reaction here, and that he was already failing to provide it.

--- Chapter 2 - Breathe ---

Breathe.
It is a strange thing when you have not done it in a while. It suddenly feels very important.
His awakening came as a jolt. His eyes snapped open and his lungs filled all at once, dragging in air as if they had forgotten how. He pulled in too much, chest hitching as his body surged ahead of him. He was lying down, though that detail lagged behind. Breathing took precedence. Cool air flooded his lungs, sharp and clean, forcing itself in with mechanical insistence.
He slowed it deliberately. In through the nose. Out through the mouth. Again. And again. The reflex eased. The pull to gasp receded. Only then did the rest of the world begin to register.
He lay beside a road, his back pressed into cold earth. Trees rose overhead, their leaves breaking sunlight into scattered patches that drifted with the breeze. Birds called somewhere nearby. The sounds had position and distance. That narrowed the possibilities.
He blinked and raised one hand into view.
The skin looked wrong. Smoother. Taut. He turned it, flexed his fingers, and watched them move immediately, without resistance.
That was his hand.
He curled his fingers again. Same response. He rolled his shoulders and noted the range of motion. The joints tracked smoothly, without the small hesitations he expected.
Placing his hands beside his hips, he pushed himself upright, moving with care learned over years of managing pain. Nothing objected. No catches. No sharp reminders. As he settled into a seated position, something brushed against his eye.
He flinched and made a short, undignified sound. His fingers caught in it and stopped.
Hair.
Running his hand through it, he checked the length and thickness. It fell back into place without argument. He spoke without planning to. “Hair.”

Interlude III - Compost
They stood at the edge of the garden and tried to understand what had gone wrong.
This was not easy, because what had gone wrong had not merely failed. It had settled in.
As Hal had once been a farm hand, he had nervously asked Alric if he could turn part of the new yard into a vegetable garden. Alric had agreed immediately and had hoped to teach Hal some of the basic principles of compost.
It had not wilted, or struggled, or produced disappointing yields that could be blamed on soil, weather, or poor planning. It had been erased with enthusiasm. What remained bore only a passing resemblance to a place where vegetables had once been asked, with some trepidation, to grow.
Rows were gone. Stakes lay half-buried at odd angles. The ground itself looked worked over, packed flat in some places and scooped hollow in others, as though the earth had been kneaded and then abandoned halfway through the process.
Beyond it, in the yard, lay the cause.
A dozen pigs sprawled across the ground in various poses of complete satisfaction. One had burrowed directly into the pile of brewing waste Alric had so carefully accumulated over winter. He gestured to a small gap where steam curled faintly into the air.
Hal leaned closer. “Clever bastard.”

Hal stared at it.
“Well,” he said, “that solves that.”
Alric nodded. “Yes. I’m not sure it solves our waste problem though or makes a new one.”

“Off you go then. I’ve got interviews. There’s Mara and Hal,” Alric said as he turned for the entrance. Mara and Hal fell in step with him as they headed for the docks.

It was the ninth and final interview that made Alric frown. Something didn’t sit right. Mara and Hal were already discussing their preferred candidate with the careful enthusiasm of people who believed they were being helpful.
They were sitting at a dockside tavern to conduct the interviews. It was pleasantly cool, the river moving lazily behind them, demonstrating the sort of patience that only came from having nowhere else to be.
A barge drifted past without anyone on deck, its mooring rope trailing in the water like an afterthought.
Alric let out a sigh.
Every candidate had been a former tavern keeper. Their answers were clean, confident, and fluent in brewing. Alric had mostly listened while Mara and Hal asked the questions they had prepared earlier, questions that had apparently been answered many times before.
Thinking it through, Mara and Hal were effective precisely because they came from other trades. Mara had brought sewing skills and made proper grain bags. Before that, Alric had tied knots and called it a day, which had worked in the same way leaning boards against things sometimes worked. A mug was set down at a nearby table, the wood dull and scarred from years of use, and possibly years of disagreement.
“Listen,” Alric said, interrupting them. “You both went through all of this. Learning to brew and everything. What part was hardest?”
They paused. Both explained it was complicated at first, with constant cleaning and timing. The boiler made some parts easier and others harder. Eventually, it became routine, which was to say survivable.
“Right. You both came from other trades. What do you think is harder. Learning from nothing, or unlearning everything?” Alric asked.
They paused again.
“Unlearning,” they answered together.
“So how easy is it to skip a step?” Alric asked quietly. “Not steaming a cask properly, because it worked before.”
The river lapped softly against the stone embankment behind them.
Both considered that and shrugged.
“See, you each brought something I didn’t have,” Alric said, shrugging himself.
“Are you saying we should turn carpenters into brewers?” Hal asked.
Alric shook his head. “I think we’re better off taking people who know nothing. Do you both know anyone who needs work?”
Mara blinked. “Are you serious?”
Alric nodded.
“I can think of thirty, easy,” Mara said. Hal nodded with her, the way people did when numbers were suddenly not theoretical.
“How many of them are honest?” Alric asked.
“Not all. But a few,” Mara said after a moment.
“And of those few,” Alric continued, “how many can you work with? Friendships get tested.”
Mara exhaled. “I see now.”
“What about Lessa?” Hal asked.
“She’s looking for work at a tavern,” Mara said, frowning.
“So? This is safer. At least ask her,” Hal said. Mara shrugged, which settled it as much as anything ever did.
Alric raised a hand. “Here’s the catch. They’ll be your juniors. Mara, you’ll be in charge of germination, grain, and bags. Hal, you’re in charge of boilers. These need to be people who can take orders from you.”
A shout from the docks cut off, replaced by the creak of rope under load, the sound of things being moved because they had to be.
There was a long silence.
“Are you promoting us?” Hal asked.
“Why not? Who has more experience running a boiler than you?” Alric said.
“Does that mean more pay? We have family responsibilities,” Hal added.
Alric nodded. That was fair. It was also, inconveniently, how things worked.
“Mister Alric, we haven’t been here that long. Are you sure?” Mara asked.
“I don’t think brewing itself is complicated,” Alric said. “What’s difficult is new stuff. New recipes. That comes with time. I’m asking you to run a process, not the whole thing.”
Mara smiled. “Alright. I’ll do my best not to make you regret it.”
“Mistakes happen,” Alric said. “Hal, anyone you’d want to work with?”
“I know a fisherman. Jory. We get on well. Times are tough. I’ll ask him.”
“Should we bring them tomorrow?” Mara asked.
“No. Tomorrow we run hard. I don’t want greenhorns in the wrong place when a boiler’s full and heating. Day after. Interviews only. I do have a condition though. Both of you have to learn to read and write. I’ll ask Seren to teach you.”
They nodded but winced at reading.
“Go talk to your people. I’ll speak to Henry and Seren. You’re not the only ones who’ll need help.”
They left in different directions. Alric stayed seated a moment longer, watching the river slide past, then stood and headed back to the warehouse.

The first person he ran into was Henry, preparing casks for the boilers. Steam curled from one as he worked the bung free, treating it with the careful confidence of someone who had learned where fingers were most often burnt.
“Henry, do you know anyone who’d want work here? Same kind of work as you.”
Henry paused. “Only my brother, Jase. Wanted to work a barge, but this is better. Honest work.”
“I can promise an interview. Day after tomorrow?”
Henry nodded.
Alric took a breath. The next conversation would be harder, mostly because it involved admitting he was wrong about something.
He found Seren in the kitchen. The space smelled faintly of boiled grain and felt humid, with clean cloths hanging to dry along one wall in a way that suggested someone had decided not to think about airflow.
“Seren, can we talk for a moment?” he asked, sitting at the table.
She looked concerned and joined him.
“First thing, I need to ask you to teach Mara and Hal how to read.” 
Seren nodded and waved her hand dismissively, waiting for him to get to the point.
“I don’t want this to come out wrong, but we need a clerk. Someone to handle accounts.”
Seren’s shoulders dropped in relief. “I agree, Mister Alric. It’s already more than I can manage once we’re running fully. And it means I can assist you properly again.”
Alric blinked. “You’re sure? You handled winter well.”
“I did fine,” Seren said. “But running fermentation, assisting you, and handling accounts once we’re supplying the whole city. It’s too much.”
A pot shifted slightly on the stove as it cooled, making a small tink noise.
Alric nodded. “Do you know anyone?”
“I do. He’s very good with numbers, but he can be strange.”
“How strange?”
“He tells people how they should be doing things. You can imagine.”
Alric found that oddly reassuring. “We don’t really have a system. He can build one properly. Ask him to come in the day after tomorrow.”
Seren nodded.
“And just to check,” Alric added, “is he stranger than me?”
Seren considered, then shook her head. “You are definitely the strangest person I know, Mister Alric.”
She said it without smiling, which made it harder to argue.
Alric winced inwardly.

Outside, an apparent homeless man tried to draw a boiler. The charcoal left heavy, uncertain lines, as if the idea itself resisted being put down. On another page, he noted that Alric had returned alone.
