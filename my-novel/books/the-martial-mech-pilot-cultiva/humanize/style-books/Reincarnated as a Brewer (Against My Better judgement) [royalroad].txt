--- Chapter 1: Meeting God ---

The first thing he noticed was that the floor was wrong.
It was smooth beneath him, but not in any way he recognised. There was no grain to it, no join, no variation in texture. It felt less like stone or wood and more like the idea of a surface. Present, functional, and entirely uninterested in explaining itself.
That alone should have been alarming. What unsettled him more was that he could feel it at all.
He had no sense of weight. No sense of limbs. No breath to draw or release. And yet the floor existed, and so did his awareness of it.
He tried to blink. Nothing happened.
“Hello! Hi there! Welcome!”
The voice was cheerful. Bright. Too close.
It didn’t arrive through sound. It simply appeared, fully formed, inside his awareness.
His attention widened, as though something inside him had finally decided to render the rest of the scene.
A building stood before him.
It was wrong in the same way the floor was wrong, but louder about it.
The roof was tiled in a style he associated with old photographs of Japan, sloping neatly and casting sharp shadows. The pillars holding it up were unmistakably Roman, fluted and proportioned with mathematical confidence. Beneath them, the floor was polished wood, warm-toned, well-kept, the sort of thing that belonged in an English pub that took its atmosphere very seriously.
Each element made sense on its own.
Together, they did not.
Floating in front of the building was a girl.
She looked to be no more than eleven. Small, slight, with one knee bent, one leg straight but neither touching the floor as though gravity were a suggestion rather than a rule.
She wore a white robe clearly meant for someone older. It draped around her in excessive folds, heavy with ceremony and authority pressed into the fabric whether it suited her or not.
The fabric shimmered faintly, points of light glinting within it like distant stars. It moved despite the lack of wind, as though aware it was being observed.
The robe was not tailored so much as imposed, worn with the careful seriousness of a child playing a role they believed they were expected to take seriously. The effect wasn’t unsettling because of what it revealed, but because of what it tried and failed to be.
It felt rehearsed.
And, like the building behind her, it didn’t quite fit.
“Hi! So I’m God!” she said brightly. “Aren’t you impressed?”
He had the distinct impression that there was a correct reaction here, and that he was already failing to provide it.

The white dimmed, folding inward like curtains being drawn. The building behind her seemed to tilt, its mismatched architecture pressing in on itself — the Roman pillars stretching too tall, the tiled roof bending at an angle that made his stomach tighten.

And then sensation crashed in all at once.

And a body that was suddenly, terrifyingly, his.

--- Chapter 2 - Breathe ---

Breathe.
It is a strange thing when you have not done it in a while. It suddenly feels very important.
His awakening came as a jolt. His eyes snapped open and his lungs filled all at once, dragging in air as if they had forgotten how. He pulled in too much, chest hitching as his body surged ahead of him. He was lying down, though that detail lagged behind. Breathing took precedence. Cool air flooded his lungs, sharp and clean, forcing itself in with mechanical insistence.
He slowed it deliberately. In through the nose. Out through the mouth. Again. And again. The reflex eased. The pull to gasp receded. Only then did the rest of the world begin to register.
He lay beside a road, his back pressed into cold earth. Trees rose overhead, their leaves breaking sunlight into scattered patches that drifted with the breeze. Birds called somewhere nearby. The sounds had position and distance. That narrowed the possibilities.
He blinked and raised one hand into view.
The skin looked wrong. Smoother. Taut. He turned it, flexed his fingers, and watched them move immediately, without resistance.
That was his hand.
He curled his fingers again. Same response. He rolled his shoulders and noted the range of motion. The joints tracked smoothly, without the small hesitations he expected.
Placing his hands beside his hips, he pushed himself upright, moving with care learned over years of managing pain. Nothing objected. No catches. No sharp reminders. As he settled into a seated position, something brushed against his eye.
He flinched and made a short, undignified sound. His fingers caught in it and stopped.
Hair.
Running his hand through it, he checked the length and thickness. It fell back into place without argument. He spoke without planning to. “Hair.”
The voice sounded wrong. Younger. Clearer.
He paused, listening to it, then began patting himself down. The next few minutes were enthusiastic poking of his own body, before he realised what he must look like to a stranger.

Orris turned and left. Light shifted as he passed beneath the open roof and out into the street, where the warehouse promptly stopped caring about him.
Two men fell in beside him as he reached the road.
“How’d it go, boss?” one asked.
“We need a meeting. All members,” Orris said, his voice tight, which suggested that several thoughts had just failed to reach a satisfying conclusion.
Back in the warehouse, ropes were being reset and the arm checked for cracks, because gravity was nothing if not consistent. Alric turned to his staff. He raised his hands, then stopped, remembering Moreen’s habit of clapping at people, and lowered them again with visible restraint.
“Alright. Let’s finish the test. Move the next barrel.”
They nodded and returned to their positions without comment, comforted by the fact that whatever had just happened was no longer their problem.
Stromni produced a drawing of the roof frame he wanted to build up. The paper was creased and darkened at the edges, weighed down at one corner by a wooden washer that had clearly been chosen for its moral authority. The structure it showed was heavy, braced, and impossible to miss from the street.
Alric studied it, eyes tracking the lines. Somewhere above them, wood knocked softly against wood as the rigging was adjusted. Everyone turned to look in the direction of the new noise.
“Do it,” he said. “Now that you’ve shown me, I’d have to remember it if someone got hurt.”
Stromni grinned. “I like the problems ya bring, lad. Don’t regret this job at all.”
“Happy staff is all that matters,” Alric said dryly.
“Oy, I’m on retainer, not your lackey,” Stromni said, shaking the paper. Laughter rippled through the group. “But aye, with how fast you lot move water, anything less will get someone killed.”
By afternoon, steam drifted lazily from the warehouse roof, thin at first, then steady enough to be seen from the street, the building quietly announcing that it was doing something important.

That evening, a man in purple with a red sash sat across from a man dressed in black. The room was narrow and well kept, its shutters closed despite the hour, as though daylight might overhear. A single oil lamp burned between them, its flame steady, the air warm but faintly stale.
The man in purple sat upright, hands folded carefully on the table. His clothing was immaculate, the colour deep and intentional, the sash marking rank rather than celebration. He had the expression of someone who was used to welcoming strangers for a living and resented how little that helped here.
“I’m a litigator for the Merchants’ Guild,” the man in black said. “You know we don’t interfere with artisans.”
“I know. But he needs to be classed as a crafter. He’s like a carpenter. Takes raw material and sells the result. Beer is no different.”
The litigator leaned back, chair legs scraping once against the floor. “Three problems. First, he already operates as a merchant, if we did this he could sell direct to the public. Second, if he’s reclassified, every inn and tavern must be as well. That means trade fees. Back pay. Twelve years’ worth, in your case. And it would be your guild paying it. Third, beer prices rise across the city, and everyone knows who caused it. Do you want that?”
He sounded bored by the end, as though this conversation had been a formality rather than a discussion.
The man in purple stared down at the table, fingers tightening against the wood. The lamp hissed softly, offering no advice.
Some time after the litigator left, the room had gone still. The man in purple turned to his assistants.
“Call a meeting. All members.”

Above the city’s third-oldest tavern is a small private sitting room, the sort with leather seats that were expensive once. Years of use have compressed them into shapes that are only comfortable to one person at a time, provided that person already knows which hollow is theirs.
On a low table rest six old tankards, some full, some empty, none quite clean, on the principle that cleaning a tankard too thoroughly dulls the flavour and, if taken far enough, the point. A fire burns low. It has burned longer than some of the men in the room have been alive. The room smells faintly of smoke, leather, old wool, and for some reason peppermint.
It is currently hosting six men. When they move, the sounds of creaking knees and popping elbows fill the space. One man shifts his weight and pauses halfway, bracing himself on the arm of his chair as if negotiating terms with his own joints. The fire settles a log and sends a brief pulse of warmth across the floor before returning to its steady burn.
The eldest grips his cane by the top third and shakes it once to make his point.
“Back in my day, the guild was easy. Thirty honest men who could sit and talk. None of this three hundred shouty business. Had respect, we did.”
He sets the cane down with finality, as though daring it to argue.
Another nods immediately. “Can’t control so many. Youngsters never listen.” He waves a hand dismissively, narrowly missing his own tankard and staring at it afterward, affronted.
The youngest of the group, the current Guild Master, exhales. “Next time we hold meetings by city district. That was a fiasco.” He lifts his tankard just enough to judge its weight before drinking, then sighs again and drinks anyway.
“Aye, makes sense,” says a man who has lifted one leg with both hands to reposition it. The movement produces an unsettling series of clicks and pops, like a bag of kindling being handled without care. He nods once when finished, satisfied.
“Well,” says another, leaning forward, “before we get into all that, my wife says mint’s good for the skin. Keeps it from cracking in winter. Says I should rub it in, mornings and nights.”
No one responds.
“You see,” the man continues, encouraged by the silence, “she says dry skin lets sickness in. Says that’s how it starts.”
The cane man clears his throat. “Beer used to be made by honest men, with grit and charcoal. I scooped it straight out the vat, and the drinker had a belly that could handle it. People have gone soft.”
“Eh?” comes from another. He squints, then settles back, content to remain deaf, which in this room is often mistaken for wisdom.
“This foreign beer,” the cane man says, spitting the word out. “What’s so special about it?”
Beneath the table, an old dog opens one eye to inspect where the spit lands. It weighs the options with professional indifference and decides against involvement without lifting its head. The fire crackles once.
“They say it tastes dwarven,” the Guild Master says. “Strong. Not sour.”
“Not sour?” says one. “No local wants that.”
The peppermint man nods at this, though it is unclear which part he agrees with. “Sour dries the skin,” he adds helpfully.
“Eh? Locals don’t know what they want,” says the cane man. Several nod. The deaf man nods a few beats later, apparently in agreement with something else entirely.
“This just another phase?” asks the man whose joints have not yet finished making their presence known. “Locals get sick of it. Come back to what they know.”
The Guild Master pauses. Someone shifts, leather creasing softly in protest. Two nods appear before he speaks, and another follows after.

The mint man leans forward again. “You mix mint with fat, see. Helps it soak in.”
