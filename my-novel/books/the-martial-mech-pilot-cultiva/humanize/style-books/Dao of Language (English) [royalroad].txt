--- Prologue ---

In the heart of China's oldest province, among mountains that hold ancient secrets, there was an archaeologist specializing in ancient languages. Her passion had taken her all over the world, and she had dedicated her life to deciphering forgotten languages and uncovering secrets buried by time. But nothing compared to the discovery she had just made.
Her latest expedition had taken her to a hidden cave, a place mentioned only in the oldest stories of the region. Inside was a majestic and enigmatic door of oriental appearance. The carved wood bore inscriptions in a language that seemed to have Chinese roots but defied any attempt at classification.
The archaeologist had spent a week in front of that door, with her assistants regularly bringing her food and fuel for the small generator that kept the lights on. Despite all her efforts, she had reached a dead end, and all that remained was to take photographs of the door and the inscriptions to analyze them in her study and consult other specialists who might be able to offer her some help.
As she approached her equipment, a slight tremor began to be felt under her feet. Without hesitation, she ran to her backpack to grab her laptop where she kept all her notes and discoveries about various languages.
In her race against time, she realized that the tremor was not natural and it seemed as if the vibrations were coming from the door itself.
With her computer in hand, she turned toward the exit, but that's when the ground beneath her gave way, throwing her into a hidden room beneath the cave just as the ceiling collapsed. Surrounded by dust and darkness, the archaeologist saw that she was now in an underground chamber, isolated from the outside world by the debris blocking the hole in the ceiling.
However, one detail caught her attention. In the center of the chamber, illuminated by a mysterious light that allowed her to see in the darkness of the cave, was another door similar to the one she had found, but with inscriptions that seemed even older.
With the mysterious light as her guide, the archaeologist searched unsuccessfully for another exit, but the only escape route she could see was the hole in the ceiling, now blocked by debris, and the enigmatic door.
Aware that every moment was precious, she approached the new door to see if it could be opened. But as she approached, despite the desperate situation, her passion for knowledge and archaeology made her heart beat faster at what lay before her. She knew that, trapped in this place, she was standing before something that could completely change the history of China, and perhaps the entire world.
As she examined the inscriptions and the door, a new tremor began to shake the cave, this time even more intense than the previous one, while the door began to glow brighter and brighter.
And as she covered her eyes from the blinding light, the ceiling, already weakened by the previous tremor, collapsed completely, burying the mysteries of the cave along with the archaeologist under tons of rock, forever.

“Come in”, my mother’s firm voice is heard from the other side.
Lianhua opens the door, and we enter to find my mother, as always, seated behind her desk while Lingxi stands beside her like a jade statue.
As soon as we enter, I hear Lianhua carefully close the door behind us, so I say “If you sent for me, I suppose my imperial brother’s delegation has already left.”
“That’s right”, my mother replies bluntly. “Jin Duwei Liang Zhen came to demand that we hand Lianhua over to him. He made every threat he could, even mentioning that he would take the matter to Tàishǒu Wu Zhānglóng, but I stuck to the plan we’d made, so there was little he could do.”
I nod slowly, as the tone of her voice makes it clear that it wasn’t a pleasant conversation.
“And what did he do when he saw he wasn’t going to get what he wanted?” I ask.
“He had no choice but to turn around”, my mother replies. “As he walked away from the palace, we saw him heading with his men toward the governor’s palace.”
I frown and look at her intently “And do you think he’ll succeed there? Or are things in the city still as bad as they were last night?”
My mother narrows her eyes, as if recalling an unpleasant report.
“Worse”, she replies. “Nei Baihu took advantage of this morning, when she sent the messengers we dispatched to the capital, to send some Nei Wei undercover to investigate. Upon their return, they reported that it appears many of the governor’s men and members of the families who entered the Celestial Fragment have not returned, and the city guard is forcibly interrogating the cultivators who left to find out what happened inside.”
Here my mother pauses before continuing “And they’re taking it as a personal attack, so they’re resisting, and there are even starting to be deaths from the most serious clashes.”
Her words hit me like a ton of bricks, but she continues “I very much doubt that, at this moment, the governor is in a position to help the delegation of the Fifteenth Imperial Prince.”
I sigh and run a hand over my face, clenching my teeth to hold back the urge to curse Ling Tian. As grateful as I am that he saved Lianhua, what he’s left in his wake is nothing short of a political and military storm.
“Thank you for updating me on how things are going”, I say to my mother, my voice calm even though a storm rages inside me. “And while we’re at it, I’d like to take this opportunity to tell you that I’m going to throw a small party to celebrate Lianhua’s return.”
My mother nods without much reaction, though her gaze hardens as she tells me “I don’t mind. But for reasons that are more than obvious, you are forbidden from leaving the palace for the time being.”
“I expected that”, I reply without resistance. “Is there anything else?”
“No. That’s all for now”, my mother says, shaking her head.
“Then, we’ll take our leave”, I say as I bow slightly. “Come on, Lianhua, take me to my room.”
When we get back to my room, Lianhua pushes my chair up to the table as the breeze comes in through the half-open window and gently rustles the curtains, filling the air with the scent of flowers.
“We still have time while Xia helps in the kitchens prepare the dishes for the party”, I say to Lianhua as I settle a little more comfortably into the chair. “So I think it’s a good time to take a look at the contents of the storage bag.”
“Then I’ll have to go get it”, she says in her usual calm tone as she nods. “I have it in my room.”
“Perfect. Then go get it”, I say, gesturing with my hand.
Without waiting for further instructions, Lianhua turns and leaves the room as the door closes behind her with a soft click, leaving me alone with my thoughts, and with anticipation bubbling in my chest like a child waiting to open Christmas presents, making me forget all about Ling Tian and the unrest.
Fortunately, it doesn’t take her long to return, and when the door opens again, she walks in with a steady stride, carrying a dark leather pouch that I recognize immediately.
The storage bag.
“Come on, don’t keep me in suspense, start taking out the contents already”, I urge her, unable to hide my excitement.
Lianhua gives me a faint, fleeting, but genuine smile. Then, she stands next to the table and begins to open the bag with expert familiarity, after which she reaches her arm inside and starts pulling out its contents.
But the first thing she pulls out leaves me a little bewildered, as it is a garment. Made of lightweight fabric, with a geometric cut, gold trim, and motifs that remind me of the frescoes of Ancient Egypt.
And after that garment, another, and yet another. And not just a few.
In no time at all, I watch in disbelief as robes, skirts, shawls, and headdresses pile up. And it’s all a mix of styles, ranging from ceremonial to practical everyday wear. Who carries all this around and decides to stash it in a magic bag as if it were the most precious thing in the world?
Fortunately, Lianhua seems to pull out what appear to be the last items of clothing, which, unfortunately, turn out to be underwear, and I notice Lianhua’s ears flush slightly as she realizes what they are, and she begins to pull out other kinds of objects.
Next is a varnished wooden case, which she opens to reveal a dozen pieces of jewelry. Bracelets, rings, and pendants made of gold and silver, all inlaid with malachite, turquoise, and emeralds. All unmistakably Egyptian-inspired, though with variations I don’t quite recognize. After showing them to me, she sets the box aside on the table, creating a sparkling corner.
Then, she takes out two small lapis lazuli tablets with inscriptions engraved in hieroglyphs of meticulous detail. She places them carefully on the table in front of me, followed by several scrolls that she sets down beside them, with the same care.
Next, I see her take out a small glass jar filled with what looks like sand, a brilliant golden color that glitters in the light streaming through the window, and she sets it on the table next to the tablets.
“And now I need a bowl”, she says suddenly.
“A bowl?” I repeat, raising an eyebrow, but she’s already heading toward the door.
She opens it just enough to step out, making sure to close it gently behind her so no one can peek inside the room.
It doesn’t take her long to return, and when she does, she’s holding a glazed ceramic bowl the size of a medium-sized basin. She places it in the center of the table with the utmost care and, without a word, opens the bag, turns it over, and places it upside down right on top of the bowl.
After that, a stream of shiny stones, all the size of a small marble, I estimate about a centimeter in diameter, begins to pour out of the bag and fall into the bowl with a dull clatter for a while.
But eventually they stop falling, and when the last stone lands, Lianhua removes the bag in a single motion.
“That’s all there is”, she tells me calmly.
I remain silent for a moment, looking at the bowl, the objects on the table, the jewelry, and finally at my clothes.
I steel myself, knowing that all of this, as interesting as it may be from an academic standpoint, isn’t of much use to me here, and there’s too much of it to hide, especially considering that I want to keep the storage bag a secret.
“Lianhua”, I say, turning my head slightly toward her. “Would you be able to get rid of the clothes discreetly and without drawing attention?”
She nods with that calm efficiency that always conveys confidence. “Yes. I can handle it without a problem.”
“Then put it away and do it as soon as you can”, I tell her before I can change my mind.
Lianhua puts the garments back into the bag one by one, folding them as if she were packing a suitcase and not disposing of a potential archaeological treasure.
Meanwhile, I look over the rest, and finally pick up one of the pendants and spin it, watching it sparkle in the light streaming through the window.
“For the jewelry”, I say as I watch the sparkles. “We’ll tell my mother you found them in the strange building along with the papers. It would be odd if you hadn’t found anything in the ruined room.”
“That’s a good decision, Zhāohuán Wen”, Lianhua replies, a hint of approval in her voice.
I look away from the pendant and set it down next to the other jewelry to point out the tablets, the jar of sand, and the bowl.
“And those? Do you know what they are?” I ask Lianhua. “Because I sense something. I can’t explain it. There’s a kind of light pressure. As if they were emitting an energy I don’t know how to interpret.”
“The stones, yes. The tablets, I suspect so, and as for the jar, I have no idea, Zhāohuán Wen”, Lianhua tells me.
“Then tell me what you know or suspect”, I say to Lianhua, with a mixture of curiosity and tension.
“The stones are spirit stones”, she tells me calmly.
My eyes widen as I pick one up from the bowl and hold it up to my eyes.
“This?” I ask, incredulous. “When I read about them, I thought they were much bigger. Or at least different.”
The stone emits a faint warmth on my fingers, barely perceptible. I return it to the bowl and look at the glittering mountain piled up inside as I say to Lianhua “And this amount? Isn’t it excessive? I thought they were scarce and very valuable.”
“They are”, Lianhua tells me without batting an eye. “The amount in that bowl is a small fortune. With it, one could buy a minor noble title in many provinces.”
