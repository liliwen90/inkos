# Chapter 1: 钢铁脉搏

The cold bit deeper than hunger.

Qin Feng pressed his face against the corrugated metal wall of the cargo container, feeling the vibration change as the freighter's engines shifted from cruise to docking thrust. His breath crystallized instantly in the recycled air. Three days since he'd crawled into this shipping crate on Kepler Station, desperate and half-dead, clutching nothing but the jade pendant that hung cold against his chest.

The pendant that should have pulsed with warm spiritual energy. Now it felt like worthless stone.

His stomach cramped—a vicious twist that doubled him over. When had he last eaten? The memory felt distant, hazy. Before the flames. Before the mountain peaks of the Kunlun Sanctuary turned to rivers of molten glass under the orbital bombardment. Before Master Chen's final words across the collapsing dimensional pocket: *"Carry the flame, young phoenix. Let it not die with us."*

Qin Feng squeezed his eyes shut, forcing the images back into dark corners. Grief was a luxury he couldn't afford. Not when his Qi reserves flickered like a dying candle, not when his body trembled from dehydration and exhaustion.

The engine note changed again. Deep thrumming resonated through the container walls. They were docking.

He struggled to his feet, legs shaking. Through a gap in the container's seal, he caught his first glimpse of Typhon Freeport.

Vast beyond comprehension. A sprawling maze of interconnected hulks and salvaged starship sections, all welded together into a single, impossible structure. Docking arms extended like mechanical spider limbs, grasping ships of every size and configuration. Neon advertisements flickered in a dozen languages he couldn't read, casting rainbow shadows across pitted hull plating.

And everywhere, mechs. Towering humanoid machines moved with purposeful grace, loading cargo, welding joints, standing guard at checkpoints. Their pilots were sealed inside gleaming cockpits, connected to their metal shells through cables and neural interfaces that looked like fever dreams made real.

*This is not the world I knew,* he thought, pressing the jade pendant harder against his palm. *But it's the world I must survive in.*

The freighter shuddered as magnetic clamps engaged. Footsteps rang on metal decking outside—heavy boots, multiple sets. Voices in the harsh, clipped syllables of Galactic Common, a language he barely understood.

"—check the manifest again. Captain says we're carrying more mass than declared—"

"—probably smugglers in the cargo sections. Always happens on the outer rim runs—"

"—bonus pay if we find stowaways. Port Authority pays well for live ones—"

Qin Feng's blood turned to ice. He pressed himself deeper into the shadows between cargo crates, trying to make himself invisible. But there was nowhere to hide in the cramped container, and his body was too weak for the concealment techniques Master Chen had taught him.

The container door groaned. Light flooded in—harsh, artificial illumination that stabbed into his dark-adapted eyes. Three figures stood silhouetted against the brightness: dock workers in stained coveralls, their faces hidden behind breath masks and work goggles.

"Well, well," said the largest of them, pulling off his mask to reveal a face mapped with scars. "What do we have here?"

Qin Feng tried to stand. His legs betrayed him. He slumped against the container wall, blinking away tears from the sudden light. "Please," he managed in broken Galactic Common. "I... I can work. I pay—"

The scarred man laughed—a sound like grinding metal. "You pay? With what, stowaway? Look at you. Skin and bones." He gestured to his companions. "Boys, we've got ourselves a genuine freeloader."

"I take nothing," Qin Feng protested, struggling to find the right words. "Only... only passage. I work for passage."

"Work?" The leader—his coveralls bore the name 'Voss' in faded letters—stepped into the container. "You can barely stand. What kind of work you think you can do?"

One of the other workers, a thin man with nervous eyes, tugged at Voss's sleeve. "Maybe we should just report him to Port Authority. Get the bounty and—"

Voss backhanded him casually. "Port Authority takes time. Paperwork." His scarred face split in an ugly grin. "But a beating? That's quick."

He pulled something from his belt—a shock baton, crackling with blue energy. "See, the thing about stowaways is they cost us. Air, food, space. Time to pay up."

Qin Feng tried to summon his Qi, to channel even a trickle of energy into his limbs. His reserves were too depleted, his body too weak. The spiritual energy that should have flowed like a river was barely a trickle.

The first blow caught him across the ribs. Electric fire exploded through his chest. He collapsed to his knees. The second strike hit his shoulder, sending convulsions down his arm.

"That's for the air," Voss said conversationally. The baton came down again. "And that's for the trouble."

Qin Feng bit back a scream, tasting blood. Pain was nothing new—Master Chen's training had included enduring hardship, pushing through suffering to reach enlightenment. But that pain had purpose, meaning. This was simply cruelty.

*I am the last flame,* he told himself as another shock coursed through his body. *I will not be extinguished by thugs.*

"Enough." Voss stepped back. "Drag him to the Port Authority lockup."

The two workers grabbed Qin Feng under the arms, hauling him upright. His legs dragged uselessly as they pulled him from the container and onto the freighter's cargo deck. Through the open bay doors, he could see the docking tube that connected to Typhon Freeport proper.

The transition hit him like a wall. Cold recycled air gave way to the station's climate-controlled atmosphere—warmer, but thick with the smell of industrial lubricants and ozone. They hauled him through the docking tube, past the customs checkpoint where bored officials barely glanced at the procession. Artificial gravity shifted subtly as they crossed from ship to station, making his already unsteady stomach lurch.

The receiving bay was chaos. Cargo mechs moved with mechanical precision, their pilots sealed in cockpits that gleamed with status lights. Qin Feng caught glimpses of clean uniforms, polished armor, weapons that hummed with barely contained energy. These people lived in a world of metal and circuitry, where strength came from machines rather than the cultivation of inner power.

A world where he was nothing.

They hauled him past loading bays and inspection stations, deeper into the station's bowels. The corridors grew narrower, the lighting dimmer. Clean efficiency gave way to jury-rigged repairs and improvised modifications. This was the Underbelly—the part of Typhon Freeport where the desperate and discarded made their homes.

Finally, they reached a section where the deck plating was little more than metal grating over empty space. The air here was thick with the smell of unwashed bodies and despair. They dumped him unceremoniously onto the grating, where he lay gasping like a landed fish.

"Welcome to paradise," Voss said, giving him a final kick to the ribs. "Try not to die too quick. Makes cleanup messy."

The dock workers departed, their laughter bouncing off the corridor walls. Qin Feng lay where they'd left him, staring up at the tangle of pipes and cables that formed the ceiling. Somewhere in the distance, he could hear the roar of a crowd, punctuated by the crash of metal on metal.

*This is rock bottom,* he thought. *This is as low as I can fall.*

Minutes passed. Maybe hours—time moved strangely in the dim corridors. His ribs ached where the shock baton had struck, and his shoulder throbbed with each heartbeat. But gradually, the immediate pain faded, leaving behind a deeper hurt. Not physical—spiritual. The wound of being utterly powerless in a world that didn't understand what he'd lost.

He pressed his palm against the jade pendant, feeling its familiar weight. Cold, yes, but still there. Still real. Master Chen had given it to him on his sixteenth birthday, carved from a piece of the mountain itself. "Remember," the old man had said, "cultivation is not about the power you wield, but the principles you embody. Strength, wisdom, perseverance—these cannot be destroyed by any force in the universe."

*Perseverance.* The word rang in his mind like a bell. Master Chen had faced impossible odds before. The Sanctuary had survived wars, natural disasters, the rise and fall of empires. And now it was gone, reduced to glass and ash. But the principles remained. The flame remained.

In him.

Footsteps approached—lighter than the dock workers', more measured. A shadow fell across him, and he looked up to see a woman crouched at his side. She was perhaps thirty years old, with sharp features and calculating eyes. Her leather jacket was worn but well-maintained, and her boots had the kind of reinforced toes that suggested she knew how to use them.

"Well," she said, her voice carrying a slight accent he couldn't place. "You're either the unluckiest tourist in the galaxy, or you're exactly what I've been looking for."

Qin Feng tried to sit up, failed, and settled for turning his head to look at her. "What... what do you want?"

"The question isn't what I want." She produced a small scanner from her jacket, running it over him while frowning at the readout. "The question is what you're willing to do to avoid starving to death in this corridor."

She showed him the scanner's display. The numbers meant nothing to him, but her expression suggested they were significant.

"Interesting." She pocketed the scanner and extended a hand. "I'm Lysandra. I find people who need opportunities and match them with opportunities that need people."

Qin Feng stared at her hand but didn't take it. "What kind of opportunities?"

Lysandra's smile was sharp as a blade. "The kind where you climb into a ten-ton war machine and try not to die for three minutes. Maybe longer if you're good."

"I don't understand."

"The Scrapyard. Down in the deep levels. Every night, pilots climb into salvaged mechs and beat the hell out of each other for the entertainment of the masses. Winners get credits. Losers get medical bills. The dead get recycled."

Qin Feng closed his eyes. "You want me to fight."

"I want you to survive. The fighting is just how you do it. See, most of the fresh meat that comes through here lasts about ninety seconds in the ring. They panic, they freeze, they get crushed. But you..." She tilted her head, studying him. "There's something in your eyes. Desperation, sure, but it's mixed with something else. Something harder."

*Steel,* he thought. *The steel Master Chen forged in me through years of training. The steel that survived the destruction of everything I knew.*

"What if I refuse?"

Lysandra shrugged. "Then you die here, on this grating, and the maintenance crews sweep your bones into the recyclers tomorrow morning. Your choice." She paused, then added more gently, "Look, I know what you're thinking. Normal work, right? Get a job in the docks, earn honest credits? Problem is, that requires documentation. Identity papers, work permits, medical clearances. You got any of those?"

Qin Feng's silence was answer enough.

"Thought so. The Scrapyard doesn't ask questions. You fight, you get paid. Simple as that."

She stood, brushing dust from her jacket. "But if you're interested in not dying, there's a fight tonight. Midnight shift. Winner gets a meal, a place to sleep, and enough credits to maybe buy a ticket off this station." Her smile returned. "Of course, first you have to learn how to pilot a mech. Most people train for months. You'll have about six hours."

She started to walk away, then paused. "Oh, and one more thing. The mech you'll be using? It's held together with prayer and scrap metal. Half the systems don't work, and the other half are trying to kill you. But it's what we've got."

Qin Feng watched her disappear into the shadows. Around him, the station hummed with mechanical life—a symphony of pumps and fans and electrical systems that never slept. In the distance, that crowd noise continued, rising and falling like ocean waves.

*A war machine,* he thought. *Ten tons of metal and circuitry, controlled through neural interface technology I don't understand.*

He pressed his hand against the jade pendant again, feeling its cold surface warm slightly under his touch. In the Sanctuary, he had learned to extend his consciousness beyond his physical form, to sense the flow of Qi through living things and inanimate objects alike. Could those same principles apply to a machine?

*Master Chen always said that cultivation was about understanding the fundamental nature of existence. Energy, matter, consciousness—all part of the same universal pattern. If that's true, then perhaps...*

He struggled to his feet, legs shaking but holding his weight. The corridor stretched in both directions, disappearing into darkness. He could follow it deeper into the station's bowels, find some forgotten corner to curl up and die in. Or he could walk back the way he'd come, find Lysandra, and step into the ring.

The pendant pulsed once against his chest—not with spiritual energy, but with the warmth of his own determination. Master Chen's voice in his memory: *"The flame is not in the pendant, young phoenix. It is in you."*

*I am the last flame,* he told himself, straightening his shoulders despite the pain. *And flames do not die quietly.*

The crowd's roar called through the station's bones, calling him toward his destiny.
