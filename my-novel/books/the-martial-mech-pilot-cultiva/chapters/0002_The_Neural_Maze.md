# Chapter 2: The Neural Maze

The Scrapyard's preparation bay stretched before Qin Feng like a mechanical graveyard. Salvaged mechs in various states of decay lined the walls, cockpits dark and lifeless. Missing limbs. Gaping torso wounds where weapons had found their mark. Each machine told a story of violence barely survived. The air hung thick with hydraulic fluid and ozone, a metallic tang that coated his throat.

Lysandra led him through the maze of twisted metal, her boots ringing against deck plating. "Welcome to paradise." She gestured at a particularly battered machine. "Your chariot awaits."

The GR-14 "Grunt" looked assembled from spare parts and held together with prayer. Olive-drab paint chipped and scarred, revealing patches of bare metal underneath. One shoulder mounted a crude projectile weapon—some kind of autocannon—while the other arm terminated in a basic manipulator claw. Eight meters tall, hunched forward like an old man carrying too heavy a burden.

"Six hours to learn what takes others months." Lysandra pulled out her scanner, studying his face. "Still think this beats starving?"

Qin Feng approached the mech slowly, running his eyes over its frame. Where others might see only rust and decay, his cultivator's training revealed deeper patterns. Stress fractures spider-webbing through armor plating. Subtle hip joint misalignment that would throw off balance. The fusion core's erratic energy signature, visible to his Spiritual Sense as a flickering flame deep in the machine's chest.

"How do I connect?"

Lysandra pointed to a cable snaking from the mech's torso to a helmet-like device hanging from a nearby rack. "Direct Neural Interface. Put on the crown, jack into the nervous system, try not to let feedback fry your brain." Her smile was sharp. "Simple."

She climbed the maintenance ladder to the cockpit, gesturing for him to follow. The pilot's seat was cramped, surrounded by displays and control surfaces that meant nothing to him. Cables and tubes hung from the ceiling like mechanical intestines, and that persistent smell of hydraulic fluid grew stronger here, concentrated.

"Basic principle." Lysandra settled the neural crown onto his head. The device pressed dozens of contact points against his scalp, heavier than expected. "DNI translates nerve impulses into machine commands. Think 'raise left arm,' mech raises left arm. Think 'fire weapon,' it fires. Trick is learning to think like a machine instead of a human."

Something activated on the control panel. The neural crown hummed to life.

Qin Feng's world exploded.

Data flooded his consciousness—weapon diagnostics, power distribution readings, structural integrity reports. Vision fractured into a dozen feeds: optical sensors, thermal imaging, electromagnetic spectrum analysis. The machine's heartbeat thundered in his ears—the steady pulse of the fusion reactor cycling through its containment field.

He screamed.

The sensation was like having his mind torn apart and scattered to the winds. Every thought became a cascading waterfall of numbers and system alerts. His body—his real body—felt distant and unreal, while the mech's massive frame pressed against his consciousness like a steel cage.

"Disconnect!" He gasped, clawing at the neural crown.

Lysandra's hands stilled his. "Easy. First time's always rough. Don't fight it. Let the data flow through you."

But Qin Feng wasn't listening. Instinctively, he did what Master Chen had taught him during early meditation training—retreated inward, pulling his consciousness back to his spiritual core. The chaotic flood receded, becoming background noise rather than overwhelming sensation.

His breathing slowed. The trembling in his hands gradually stilled as he applied the Four Pillars breathing technique, each inhalation drawing calm from his center, each exhalation releasing the chaos. The acrid smell of ozone mixed with his own sweat, grounding him in physical sensation.

In the sudden quiet, he extended his Spiritual Sense.

The change was immediate and profound. Where before he had perceived only mechanical chaos, now he could sense the mech's true nature. The fusion core wasn't just a power source—it was a heart, pumping energy through conduits that resembled spiritual meridians. Hydraulic systems were muscles, sensor arrays were eyes and ears, the structural frame was bones.

*It's alive. Not truly alive, but... structured like life. Energy flowing through pathways, systems working in harmony. Like a body made of steel and circuitry.*

"Better?" Lysandra asked.

Qin Feng nodded slowly, residual disorientation fading as his cultivated mental discipline reasserted itself. The data feeds remained manageable now, providing information rather than drowning him. He could feel the machine's weight distribution, the slight lag in the left knee actuator, the way the autocannon's targeting system drifted rightward.

"Now let's see if you can make it move."

The first attempts were clumsy. Thinking about raising the mech's left arm, the massive limb jerked upward with mechanical precision. But there was no grace, no flow. Each movement was isolated, disconnected from the whole.

*Wrong approach. I'm thinking like a puppeteer controlling a marionette. But cultivation teaches that the body is one unified system. Every movement flows from the center, from the dantian.*

He focused on the fusion core, treating it as the mech's spiritual center. Instead of commanding individual limbs, he imagined energy flowing outward from that central point, animating the entire frame. 

The difference was immediate.

The mech's movements became fluid, natural. When he thought about taking a step, the machine didn't just lift its foot—it shifted weight, adjusted balance, moved with coordinated grace. Hydraulic systems sang in harmony instead of grinding against each other, their mechanical chorus mixing with the ever-present smell of fluid and ozone.

"Impressive." Lysandra murmured, making notes on her scanner. "Most rookies spend their first hour learning to walk without falling over. You're already moving like you've been doing this for weeks."

Qin Feng didn't respond, too focused on inhabiting the mech's frame. It was similar to the body-enhancement techniques Master Chen had taught him—extending awareness beyond physical form, treating tools and weapons as extensions of will. But this was more profound. The mech wasn't just a tool; it was becoming part of him.

Through the machine's sensors, he could perceive the preparation bay in perfect detail. Thermal signatures of other pilots working on their mechs. Electromagnetic emissions from various power sources. Subtle vibrations in deck plating as heavy machinery moved elsewhere in the station.

And something else. A flaw in the fusion core's containment field, invisible to diagnostic systems but clear to his Spiritual Sense. The reactor was slowly losing efficiency, bleeding energy into surrounding structure. Left unchecked, it would either shut down during combat or suffer catastrophic failure.

*I could fix it. A tiny amount of Qi, channeled through the power conduits, could stabilize the containment field.*

The calculation was brutal but clear. His Qi reserves were critically low—three days without food or proper rest had left him running on spiritual fumes. But the mathematics were simple: a mech failure during combat meant certain death, while Qi depletion meant weakness and vulnerability. Death was worse than weakness.

More importantly, this wasn't reckless expenditure. The repair required precision, not power—a surgeon's touch rather than a warrior's strength. He could manage it with minimal energy if he was careful.

Qin Feng made his decision. Carefully, so carefully, he extended a thread of spiritual energy into the machine's power system. The sensation was like performing surgery with bare hands—one wrong move could destroy both him and the mech. He found the fluctuation in the containment field, gently smoothed it, using his Qi to reinforce the magnetic bottles that held the fusion reaction in check.

The effort left him gasping, hands trembling as the last of his reserves drained away. Sweat poured down his face, and his vision blurred at the edges. But the mech's power output stabilized, erratic fluctuations evening into steady rhythm.

"Qin?" Lysandra's voice carried concern. "You okay? Your vitals just spiked."

He forced himself to focus, drawing on breathing techniques to steady his voice. "Fine. Just... adjusting to the interface."

She frowned at her scanner readings. "Weird. The mech's efficiency just improved. Diagnostic systems must have auto-corrected something." A shrug. "Lucky break. This old heap needs all the help it can get."

*Lucky break. If only she knew.*

The next few hours passed in intensive training, the smell of hydraulic fluid becoming as familiar as incense in a temple. Lysandra put him through basic movement drills—walking, running, climbing over obstacles. Then weapons training with the autocannon, teaching him to acquire targets and compensate for the weapon's rightward drift. Finally, basic combat maneuvers—grappling with another mech, using environment to advantage, falling without damaging critical systems.

Through it all, Qin Feng found himself translating every lesson into cultivation terms. The autocannon wasn't just a weapon—it was an extension of will, like the sword forms Master Chen had taught him. Grappling techniques weren't mere wrestling—they were applications of leverage and momentum, the same principles governing internal energy manipulation.

After five grueling hours, with barely sixty minutes until midnight, Lysandra called a halt to the training. He was moving the Grunt with surprising fluency now. Not perfect—there were still moments of awkwardness, times when human reflexes clashed with machine capabilities. But good enough to survive, perhaps.

"Not bad." Lysandra admitted, reviewing her notes. "You've got natural talent. Most people take weeks to achieve this level of integration." She paused, studying him with calculating eyes. "Course, talent only gets you so far. In the ring, you'll face pilots with years of experience. They'll know tricks you haven't dreamed of."

"What kind of tricks?"

"Psychological warfare, for one. They'll try to rattle you, make you lose focus. Some have illegal mods—neural boosters, reflex enhancers, things that give them an edge the DNI crown can't match." She closed her scanner with a snap. "And then there's the simple fact that their mechs are better than yours. Newer weapons, stronger armor, more sophisticated targeting systems."

Qin Feng absorbed this information silently. In the cultivation world, facing superior opponents was nothing new. The key was understanding your own advantages and using them wisely.

"Tell me about tonight's fight."

Lysandra consulted her notes. "Three-way match. You, a veteran named Kozlov, and a newcomer called Reaper. Kozlov's been fighting in the Scrapyard for two years—tough but predictable. Likes to use his mech's superior firepower to keep opponents at range. Reaper's an unknown quantity. Showed up last week with a heavily modified brawler-class mech. Fast, aggressive, probably augmented."

"The arena?"

"Debris field. Lots of cover, multiple levels. The crowd likes it when pilots get creative with the environment." Her smile turned predatory. "Course, creativity can get you killed if you're not careful. Last month, someone tried to use a crane as a weapon. Ended up crushed when it collapsed."

Qin Feng nodded. He could work with a debris field. His Spiritual Sense would give him perfect awareness of terrain, allowing him to navigate obstacles that would trip up pilots relying solely on optical sensors.

"One more thing." Lysandra's tone grew serious. "The Scrapyard has rules, but they're flexible. Officially, it's non-lethal combat. Disabled mechs are supposed to yield. Unofficially?" She shrugged. "Accidents happen. Weapons malfunction. Reactors overload. The crowd doesn't pay to see people walk away unharmed."

The implications hung in the air between them, mixing with the persistent smell of hydraulic fluid and ozone. This wasn't just a test of skill—it was a matter of life and death.

"I understand," Qin Feng said quietly.

"Do you?" Lysandra leaned forward, studying his face intently. "Because once you step into that ring, there's no backing out. No mercy, no second chances. You either win, or you become another cautionary tale."

Qin Feng thought of Master Chen's final words, of the jade pendant that lay cold against his chest, of the flames that had consumed everything he'd ever known. He had already lost everything once. He would not lose again.

"I understand." This time his voice carried the weight of absolute conviction.

Lysandra held his gaze for a long moment, then nodded slowly. "Good. Get some rest. The fight starts at midnight, and you'll need every advantage you can get."

She walked away, leaving Qin Feng alone in the cockpit, feeling the mech's systems humming around him. The machine was no longer alien—it was becoming familiar, almost comfortable. Like putting on well-worn clothes or picking up a favored weapon.

*Master Chen always said that cultivation was about understanding the fundamental nature of existence. Energy, matter, consciousness—all part of the same universal pattern. If that's true, then perhaps there's no real difference between flesh and steel, between biological life and mechanical function. Perhaps they're just different expressions of the same underlying principles.*

The thought was both exciting and terrifying. If he was right, then the potential applications were limitless. But if he was wrong, or if he pushed too hard too fast, he could destroy himself and everything he was trying to protect.

The fusion core pulsed steadily in the mech's chest, its rhythm almost matching his own heartbeat. In less than an hour, he would climb back into this cockpit and risk everything on a single fight. Win, and he would have food, shelter, and the credits he needed to survive in this strange new world. Lose, and he would join the ranks of the forgotten dead, another casualty in the endless hunger for entertainment.

*I am the last flame. And flames do not die quietly.*

Outside the cockpit, the Scrapyard continued its preparations for the night's festivities. Mechanics worked on damaged mechs, welding patches over holes and replacing blown actuators. Other pilots ran through their own training routines, their machines moving with the practiced efficiency of long experience.

None of them knew that they were about to witness something unprecedented—the first step in the resurrection of a lost art, the marriage of ancient wisdom and modern technology. None of them could have guessed that the starving refugee in the battered Grunt would soon change everything they thought they knew about the nature of power itself.

But they would learn. Tonight, in the ring, they would see what happened when ten thousand years of cultivation met the cutting edge of galactic civilization.

The last flame was about to ignite.
